Portfolio Thesis

Discipline at the Point of Maximum Fear

The Risk Mitigator Flex (RMF) strategy is built on a single conviction: the best time to own equities is when no one else wants them. Rather than holding a static allocation through a crisis, RMF enters conservatively — anchored in short-duration bonds and precious metals — then systematically migrates capital into equities at pre-defined market drawdown levels. Each step is rules-based and removes the emotional friction that causes most investors to sell at the bottom.

The following hypothetical backtest applies RMF's published migration rules to the most severe market dislocation in modern history — the Global Financial Crisis of 2007–2009 — using actual fund-level returns from DFA, Vanguard, and spot precious metals pricing. Two profiles are presented: Balanced (for clients in or near retirement) and Aggressive (for clients with a long time horizon and no income dependence).

Strategy Deep Dive

Migration Mechanics & Performance

The Balanced strategy sells bonds and metals into equities at each migration step, ending at 48% equity / 40% bond / 12% metals. Designed for clients who need a floor on drawdown.

Key Takeaway

The RMF Balanced strategy held its drawdown to −22.7% while global equity investors (VT) lost 57% peak-to-trough. It was above its starting value by May 2010 — more than 10% above — when VT investors were still down 13%. For clients in or near retirement, the −22.7% floor versus a −57% experience is the entire point of the strategy.

The Aggressive strategy never sells metals — all three migration steps liquidate bonds only, ending at 80% equity / 20% metals / 0% bonds. Designed for clients with a long runway and no income dependence.

The Right Fit

Balanced is built for the client who needs to sleep at night during a crisis — a −22.7% floor vs. −57% for a global equity portfolio. Aggressive is built for the client with decades ahead and the conviction to hold through a deeper trough for a substantially larger payoff. Both strategies share the same structural advantage: they buy equities at the worst possible moment for the market, which is the best possible moment for the investor.
